# Cho Man-sik 

Affiliation: Korean Social Democratic Party

![Cho Man-sik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:FilePath/Cho_Man-sik_02.jpg)

Cho Man-sik was born in 1882 in Pyongyang, raised on the dual pillars of Korean tradition and Presbyterian faith. He came to embody the virtues of duty, sobriety, and integrity, earning the title “the Gandhi of Korea.” For Cho, independence was not only a political program but a moral mission: the salvation of a people through righteousness and national solidarity. He lived modestly, taught, organized, and led boycotts, inspiring thousands by example.

Yet Cho was no simple dreamer. Unlike many Christian nationalists of his time, he refused to dismiss the appeal of socialism. To him, the true dividing line was not left versus right, but Korean versus foreign. He was willing to work with Communists as long as they were Korean patriots, not exiles flown in from Moscow or men parroting orders from abroad. His openness to coalition made him seem pragmatic to some, dangerously naïve to others. Where Rhee saw betrayal, and Pak saw class struggle, Cho clung to a stubborn belief that Koreans could govern themselves together, if only they put Korea first.

That belief left him isolated. Nationalists saw him as too soft, Communists saw him as too bourgeois, and foreign powers saw little use for a man who refused to bend. Still, Cho endured as a moral beacon, admired but seldom heeded. His refusal to compromise principle for expedience ensured his purity — and his irrelevance. In a Korea ruled by prophets, martyrs, and revolutionaries, Cho Man-sik remained the moralist: respected, but sidelined, always a conscience, never a commander.
